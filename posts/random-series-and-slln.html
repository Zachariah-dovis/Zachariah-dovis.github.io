Random Series and SLLN

Random Series

In calculus, we study the convergence properties of series. Similarly, in probability theory, we are interested in the convergence of series of random variables. Consider the series:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{Z_n}{n} \]

where each \(Z_n\) is an independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variable taking values in \(\{ -1, 1 \}\) with equal probability (i.e., \(\mathbb P(Z_n=1)=\mathbb P(Z_n=−1)=\frac 12\)).

Question: How can we determine the convergence properties of this series? Specifically, does the series converge almost surely, in mean square, or in distribution?

To answer the question, we prove the following theorem:

Theorem 1(Three-series theorem). Let \(X_1, X_2, . . .\) be independent. Let \(A > 0\) and \(Y_n = X_n\mathbb 1_{\{|X_n|≤A\}}\). In order for \(\sum_n X_n\) to converge a.s., it is necessary and sufficient that:

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty\mathbb{P}[|X_n|>A]<+\infty,\tag{1} \]

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty\mathbb{E}[Y_n]\text{ converges},\tag{2} \]

and

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty\mathrm{Var}[Y_n]<+\infty.\tag{3} \]

Remark. This theorem controls the convergence of \(X_n\) via a real number \(A\) and another series \(Y_n\), we could simply view (1) as the condition to control the terms in \(X_n\) which is greater than \(A\).

Sufficiency

Lemma 2(First Borel-Cantelli lemma (BC1)). Let \((A_n)_n\) be a sequence of events. If

\[ \sum_n\mathbb{P}[A_n]<+\infty, \]

then

\[ \mathbb{P}[A_n\text{ i.}o.]=0. \]

Proof. We claim it suffices to prove that

\[ \sum_n(Y_n-\mathbb{E}[Y_n])\text{ converges.} \]

Indeed, suppose that this is the case, then by (2) it follows that

\[ \sum_nY_n\text{ converges.} \]

By (1) and (BC1),

\[ \sum_nX_n\text{ converges.} \]

In order to prove the claim, we show the following:

Theorem 3(Zero-mean series theorem). Let \(X_1, X_2, . . .\) be independent with mean \(0\). If

\[ \sum_n\mathrm{Var}[X_n]<+\infty \]

then \(S_n = \sum_{k\le n} X_k\) converges a.s.

Proof. For \(M > 0\), let

\[ w_M\equiv\sup_{n,m\geq M}|S_n-S_m|\downarrow w_\infty. \]

By a Cauchy-type argument it suffices to prove that, for all \(\epsilon > 0\),

\[ \mathbb{P}[w_\infty>2\varepsilon]=\mathbb{P}[\cap_M\{w_M>2\varepsilon\}]=\lim_M\mathbb{P}[w_M>2\varepsilon]=0, \]

To argue about the sup we prove the following:

Theorem 4(Kolmogorov’s maximal inequality). Let \(X_1, X_2, . . .\) be independent with \(0\) mean and finite variance. Then

\[ \mathbb{P}\left[\max_{1\leq k\leq n}|S_k|\geq x\right]\leq\frac{\mathrm{Var}[S_n]}{x^2}. \]

Note that this is a strengthening of Chebyshev’s inequality for sums of independent RVs.

Proof. The trick is to divide the event of interest into when it first occurs. Let

\[ A_k=\{|S_k|\geq x\text{ but }|S_j|<x\text{ for }j<k\}. \]

Then, using independence of \(S_k\mathbb 1_{A_k}\) and \((S_n−S_k)\) and the fact that \(\mathbb E[S_n−S_k] = 0\),

\[ \begin{aligned}\mathbb{E}[S_n^2]& \geq\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[S_n^2;A_k] \\&=\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[S_k^2+2S_k(S_n-S_k)+(S_n-S_k)^2;A_k] \\&\geq\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[S_k^2;A_k] \\&\geq\sum_{k=1}^nx^2\mathbb{P}[A_k] \\ &\ge x^2\mathbb{P}\left[\max_{1\leq k\leq n}|S_k|\geq x\right].\end{aligned} \]

Going back to the previous proof, note that Kolmogorov’s maximal inequality implies

\[ \mathbb{P}\left[\max_{M\leq m\leq N}|S_m-S_M|>\varepsilon\right]\leq\frac{\mathrm{Var}[S_N-S_M]}{\varepsilon^2}\leq\frac{\sum_{m\geq M+1}\mathrm{Var}[X_m]}{\varepsilon^2}, \]

so taking a limit when \(N \to +\infty\) then \(M \to +\infty\) and using continuity

\[ \mathbb{P}\left[\sup_{m\geq M}|S_m-S_M|>\varepsilon\right]\to0,\quad\mathrm{as~}M\to+\infty. \]

Finally, note that

\[ \mathbb{P}[w_M>2\varepsilon]\leq\mathbb{P}\left[\sup_{m\geq M}|S_m-S_M|>\varepsilon\right]\to0, \]

using \(|S_n − S_m| \leq |S_n − S_M| + |S_m − S_M|\).

Necessity

Lemma 5(Second Borel-Cantelli lemma (BC2)). If the events \((A_n)_n\) are independent, then \(\sum_n\mathbb P[A_n] = +\infty\) implies \(\mathbb P[A_n\ i.o.] = 1\).

Proof. Suppose \(\sum_n X_n\) converges. Then \(X_n \to 0\) and by (BC2), for all \(A > 0\), (1) holds and \(\sum_n Y_n\) converges as well. We use symmetrization. If \(Z\) is a RV and \(\tilde Z\) is an independent copy. Then we let \(Z^\circ = Z −\tilde Z\). We will show below that the convergence of a zero-mean, uniformly bounded random series implies the convergence of the second moments. Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_nY_n^\circ\text{ converges}& \Longrightarrow\sum_n\mathrm{Var}[Y_n]=\frac12\sum_n\mathrm{Var}[Y_n^\circ]<+\infty \\ &\Longrightarrow\sum_n(Y_n-\mathbb{E}[Y_n])\text{ converges} \\ &\Longrightarrow\sum_n\mathbb{E}[Y_n]\text{ converges.} \end{aligned} \]

The statement above will follow from the following maximal inequality:

Theorem 6. Let \(X_1, X_2, . . .\) be independent with zero mean, finite variance and \(\mathbb P[X_i \leq c] = 1\) for some \(c > 0\). Then

\[ \mathbb{P}\left[\max_{1\leq k\leq n}|S_k|\geq x\right]\geq1-\frac{(c+x)^2}{\mathrm{Var}[S_n]}. \]

Proof. Let \(A\) be the event above and define \(A_k\) as before. On the one hand, noting that on \(A_k\) we have \(|S_k| \leq c + x\) and arguing as before,

\[ \begin{aligned}\mathbb{E}[S_{n}^{2};A]& =\sum_k\mathbb{E}[S_k^2+(S_n-S_k)^2;A_k] \\&\leq\mathbb{P}[A]\left((c+x)^2+\sum_{k=1}^n\mathbb{E}[X_k^2]\right) \\&=\mathbb{P}[A]\left((c+x)^2+\mathbb{E}[S_n^2]\right).\end{aligned} \]

On the other hand,

\[ \mathbb{E}[S_n^2;A]=\mathbb{E}[S_n^2]-\mathbb{E}[S_n^2;A^c]\geq\mathbb{E}[S_n^2]-x^2\mathbb{P}[A^c]. \]

Rearranging gives the result.

Finally if we had \(\sum_n \text{Var}[Y_n] = +\infty\), the previous inequality (used as before) would give

\[ \mathbb{P}\left[\sup_{k\geq M}|S_n-S_M|>\varepsilon\right]=1, \]

for all \(M > 0\)—a contradiction!

Finally, we conclude the proof of Theorem 1.

Now we could solve the problem at the beginning as follows:

Take \(A > 1\). Then \(X_n = Y_n = n^{−1}Z_n\) and \(\mathbb E[Y_n] = 0\) for all \(n\). Therefore it suffices to check (3). Note that

\[ \sum_n\mathrm{Var}[X_n]=\sum_n\mathbb{E}[X_n^2]=\sum_n\frac1{n^2}<+\infty. \]

Theorefore, the random series converge almost surely.

A Second Proof of the SLLN

An interesting application of random series is that it could give us a proof of Strong Law Large Numbers which is different from the classical proof.

We begin with the following lemma:

Lemma 7(Kronecker’s lemma). If \(a_n \uparrow +\infty\) and \(\sum_n \frac{x_n}{a_n}\) converges then

\[ \frac1{a_n}\sum_{m=1}^nx_m\to0. \]

Proof. Let \(b_m = \sum^m_{k=1} x_k/a_k\). Noting that

\[ x_m=a_m(b_m-b_{m-1}), \]

we have (setting \(a_0=b_0=0\))

\[ \begin{aligned}\frac1{a_n}\sum_{m=1}^nx_m& =\frac1{a_n}\left(\sum_{m=1}^na_mb_m-\sum_{m=1}^na_mb_{m-1}\right) \\&=\frac1{a_n}\left(a_nb_n+\sum_{m=1}^na_{m-1}b_{m-1}-\sum_{m=1}^na_mb_{m-1}\right) \\&=b_n-\sum_{m=1}^n\frac{a_m-a_{m-1}}{a_n}b_{m-1}.\end{aligned} \]

Since \(b_n \to b_\infty < +\infty\) and the \(a_n\)’s are non-decreasing, the average on the RHS converges to \(b_\infty\).

Now, we are ready to give the proof of SLLN.

Theorem 8(Strong Law of Large Numbers). Let \(X_1, X_2, . . .\) be pairwise independent IID with \(\mathbb E|X_1| < +\infty\). Let \(S_n = \sum_{k\leq n} X_k\) and \(\mu =\mathbb E[X_1]\). Then

\[ \frac{S_n}n\to\mu,\quad a.s. \]

Proof. We would apply the following simple Lemma:

Lemma 9. If \(Y \geq 0\) and \(p > 0\), then

\[ \mathbb E[Y^p]=\int_0^\infty py^{p-1}\mathbb{P}[Y>y]\mathrm{d}y. \]

Let \(Y_k = X_k\mathbb 1_{\{|X_k|\le k\}}\) and \(T_n = \sum_{k\le n} Y_k\). (Note that the \(Y_i\)’s are not identically distributed.) By integrating and using the above Lemma,

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{+\infty}\mathbb{P}[|X_k|>k]\leq\mathbb{E}|X_1|<+\infty, \]

(BC1) implies that it suffices to prove \(n^{−1}T_n \to \mu\).

Now, we claim that

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac{\mathrm{Var}[Y_i]}{i^2}\leq\mathbb{E}|X_1|<+\infty. \]

Proof. By Fubini’s theorem

\[ \begin{aligned}\sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac{\mathrm{Var}[Y_i]}{i^2}& \leq\sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i^2]}{i^2} \\&=\sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac1{i^2}\int_0^\infty2y\mathbb{P}[|Y_i|>y]\mathrm{d}y \\&=\sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac1{i^2}\int_0^\infty\mathbb{1}_{\{y\leq i\}}2y\mathbb{P}[|Y_i|>y]\mathrm{d}y \\&=\int_0^\infty\left(2y\sum_{i=1}^{+\infty}\frac1{i^2}\mathbb{1}_{\{y\leq i\}}\right)\mathbb{P}[|Y_i|>y]\mathrm{d}y \\&\leq\int_0^\infty C^{\prime}\mathbb{P}[|Y_i|>y]\mathrm{d}y \\&\leq C^{\prime}\mathbb{E}|X_1|,\end{aligned} \]

where the second to last inequality follows by integrating.

This implies, from Theorem 3, (or from the Three-Series Theorem with \(A > 2\)),

\[ \sum_k\frac{Y_k-\mathbb{E}[Y_k]}k\quad\text{converges a.s.} \]

From a simple calculation we prove \(n^{−1}T_n \to \mu\), thereby concluding the proof of SLLN.
